INTRODUCTION

It is one of the basic tissues which connects and gives structural and metabolic support to the other tissues and organs of body.

GENERAL FEATURES

It is made up of cells, fibers and ground substance.

Fibers and ground substance collectively, are called extra or intercellular matrix which forms the major constituents. Embryonic connective tissue is called mesenchyme.

COMPONENTS

Cells

Connective tissue cells are of two types:

  1. Fixed or intrinsic cells, e.g., Fibroblasts and fibrocytes, undifferentiated mesenchymal cells, fat cells or adipocytes and macrophages or histiocytes

  2. Free or extrinsic or wandering cells, e.g., Free macrophages, plasma cells, mast cells and leucocytes migrated from the blood

Connective tissue fibers are of three types:

  1. Collagen

  2. Elastic

  3. Reticular

Connective tissue ground substance is a gel-like material in which cells and fibers are embedded.

Fibroblasts: Most numerous, flat and fusiform cells with processes. Cell membrane is ill-defined and cytoplasm is basophilic. Nucleus is large, oval, euchromatic with prominent nucleolus. These cells form connective tissue fibers and ground substance. When they form reticular fibers, then, they are called reticular cells. Specialized contractile fibroblasts are called myofibroblasts. Old inactive fibroblasts are called fibrocytes. Fibroblasts are distributed in all types of connective tissues.33

35Fat or adipose cells or adipocytes or lipocytes: These are large, oval or spheroidal cells containing a large droplet or globule of fat. This fat is dissolved by xylene during processing for slide preparation, leaving a large empty space, a thin rim of cytoplasm and a peripheral nucleus giving a characteristic signet ring appearance. They store energy (lipid). The cells aggregate to form the adipose tissue. The cells are supported by the reticular fibers. It is distributed in most of the connective tissues especially in adipose tissue.

Fig. 3 Adipocyte (To be drawn by students).

36Macrophages or histiocytes or clasmatocytes: These are irregular cells with short blunt processes. Cytoplasm is acidophilic granular and vacuolated. Sometimes vacuoles show ingested particles. Nucleus is round or indented, eccentric and deeply stained. Macrophages are derived from the blood monocytes. They are phagocytic in nature (engulf foreign particles) and are part of mononuclear phagocytic system or reticuloendothelial system. When foreign body is large, many macrophages group around and fuse to form a multinucleated giant cell. They can be stained by vital stains like Trypan blue or India ink. During inflammatory or antigenic stimulation, the fixed macrophages can withdraw their processes and become free macrophages. It is seen in most of the connective tissues, especially in loose connective tissue.

Fig. 4 Macrophage (To be drawn by students).

37Mast cells: These are round or fusiform with centrally placed round nucleus. Cytoplasm is packed with metachromatic granules containing histamine and heparin (metachromasia means showing purple staining with blue dyes like Toluidine blue or Alcian blue). Functionally, they resemble blood basophils hence also called Connective Tissue basophils. They release histamine in response to antigens in inflammatory, allergic and hypersensitive states. Anaphylaxis is a severe allergic or hypersensitive state. There are two types of mast cells (i) connective tissue mast cell found in skin and peritoneal cavity and is larger (10–12 μm) and cytoplasmic granules contain heparin and (ii) mucosal mast cell found in the lamina propria of intestine and lung and is smaller (5–10 μm) and cytoplasmic granules contain chondroitin sulphate instead of heparin. They are distributed along small blood vessels, hence also called adventitial cells.

Fig. 5 Mast cell (To be drawn by students).

38Plasma cells/Plasmatocytes: These cells are oval or round. Cytoplasm is basophilic. Nucleus is eccentric showing clumps of chromatin around the periphery of nucleus giving a characteristic cartwheel appearance. They are derived from the B lymphocytes. They produce antibodies (immunoglobulins) which are temporary stored in large vacuoles called ‘Russell bodies’. It is distributed in lamina propria of gastrointestinal, respiratory and urinary system where they form immunological barrier along with lymphocytes against invasion of pathogens.

Fig. 6 Plasma cell (To be drawn by students).

39Pigment cells: They are stellate shaped cells with long cytoplasmic processes. Cytoplasm contains brown pigments called melanin. It is distributed in skin, iris and choroid of eye and pia mater.

Fig. 7 Pigment cell (To be drawn by students).

40Leucocytes migrated from blood: They exhibit amoeboid movements and phagocytosis or immune response during inflammatory conditions. They are of two types: (i) Granular leucocytes (neutrophils, eosinophils and basophils) and (ii) Mononuclear leucocytes (lymphocytes and monocytes).

Connective Tissue Fibers

Collagen fibers

Most numerous and widely distributed, white in color. Present singly or in bundles. Individual fibers do not branch but a number of fibers in group leave a bundle and either run independently or join another bundle. Fibers are strong, flexible, inelastic or inextensible and acidophilic. Each fiber consists of small parallel fibrils of an albuminous protein called ‘collagen’. Fibers are produced by the fibroblasts. It is distributed in all CT especially in bones, ligaments and tendons.

Fig. 11 Collagen fibers (To be drawn by students).

44Reticular (Reticulin) fibers

Structurally similar to collagen fibers. They are very thin, small and immature collagen fibers. They run singly, branch and anastomose freely and forming a supportive fine framework or meshwork called reticulum. Non-stainable with routine Hematoxylin-Eosin (H&E) stains but takes blackish staining with special stain silver salts and so called ‘argyrophilic or argentophil’ fibers. Fibers are produced by reticular cells. It is distributed in supportive framework of lymphoid and glandular tissues especially in endocrine glands.

Fig. 12 Reticular fibers (To be drawn by students).

45Elastic fibers

Fewer in number and yellow in color. Run singly, branch and anastomose freely forming a network. Fibers are elastic, stretchable or extensible. They are produced by the fibroblasts and formed by a protein called ‘elastin’. They are non-stainable with H&E stains but take brown staining with special stain called Orcein. It is distributed in ligamentum nuchae, ligamentum flava, large arteries, etc.

Clinical Correlation/Vertical Integration

  • Regeneration of connective tissue: The healing of surgical incisions depends on the reparative capacity of connective tissue. The main cell type involved in repair is the fibroblast. When it is adequately stimulated, such as during wound healing, the fibrocyte reverts to the fibroblast state, and its synthetic activities are reactivated. In such instances the cell reassumes the form and appearance of a fibroblast.

  • Wound healing: The myofibroblast, a cell with features of both fibroblasts and smooth muscle, is also observed during wound healing. These cells have most of the morphological characteristics of fibroblasts but contain increased amounts of actin microfilaments and myosin and behave like smooth muscle cells. Their activity is responsible for wound closure after tissue injury, a process called wound contraction.

  • Macrophages: When adequately stimulated, macrophages may increase in size and are arranged in clusters forming epithelioid cells (named for their vague resemblance to epithelial cells), or several may fuse to form multinuclear giant cells. Both cell types are usually found only in pathological conditions. Macrophages act as defense elements. They phagocytose cell debris, abnormal extracellular matrix elements, neoplastic cells, bacteria, and inert elements that penetrate the organism. Macrophages also have an important role in removing cell debris and damaged extracellular components formed during the physiological involution process. For example, during pregnancy the uterus increases in size. Immediately after parturition, the uterus suffers an involution during which some of its tissues are destroyed by the action of macrophages. Macrophages are also secretory cells that produce an impressive array of substances, including enzymes (e.g., collagenase) and cytokines that participate in defensive and reparative functions, and they exhibit increased tumor cell–killing capacity.

  • Mast cells: Release of the chemical mediators stored in mast cells promotes the allergic reactions known as immediate hypersensitivity reactions, because they occur within a few minutes after penetration by an antigen of an individual previously sensitized to the same or a very similar antigen. There are many examples of immediate hypersensitivity reaction; a dramatic one is anaphylactic shock, a potentially fatal condition. The process of anaphylaxis consists of the following sequential events: The first exposure to an antigen (allergen), such as bee venom, results in production of the IgE class of immunoglobulins (antibodies) by plasma cells. IgE is avidly bound to the surface of mast cells. A second exposure to the antigen results in binding of the antigen to IgE on the mast cells. This event triggers release of the mast cell granules, liberating histamine, leukotrienes, ECF-A, and heparin. Histamine causes contraction of smooth muscle (mainly of the bronchioles) and dilates and increases permeability (mainly in postcapillary venules). Any liberated histamine is inactivated immediately after release. Leukotrienes produce slow contractions in smooth muscle, and ECF-A attracts blood eosinophils. Heparin is a blood anticoagulant, but blood clotting remains normal in humans during anaphylactic shock. Mast cells are widespread in the human body but are particularly abundant in the dermis and in the digestive and respiratory tracts.

  • Plasma cells: They are derived from B lymphocytes and are responsible for the synthesis of antibodies. Antibodies are immunoglobulins produced in response to penetration by antigens. Each antibody is specific for the one antigen that gave rise to its production and reacts specifically with molecules possessing similar epitopes. The results of the antibody–antigen reaction are variable. The capacity of the reaction to neutralize harmful effects caused by antigens is important. An antigen that is a toxin (e.g., tetanus, diphtheria) may lose its capacity to do harm when it combines with its respective antibody.

  • Disorders of collagen synthesis: Collagen synthesis depends on the expression of several genes and several post-translational events. Certain mutations in the COL1A1 and COL1A2 genes lead to osteogenesis imperfecta. However, a single amino acid change is sufficient to cause certain forms of this disease, particularly mutations involving glycine. Glycine must be at every third position for the collagen triple helix to form.

    In addition to these disorders, several diseases result from an overaccumulation of collagen. In progressive systemic sclerosis, almost all organs may present an excessive accumulation of collagen (fibrosis). This occurs mainly in the skin, digestive tract, muscles, and kidneys, causing hardening and functional impairment of the implicated organs. Keloid is a local swelling caused by abnormal amounts of collagen that form in scars of the skin. Keloids, which occur most often in individuals of black African descent, can be a troublesome clinical problem to manage; not only can they be disfiguring, but excision is almost always followed by recurrence.51

  • Ehlers–Danlos type IV disease, a deficiency of collagen type III, is characterized by ruptures in arteries and the intestine, both structures rich in reticular fibers.

  • Marfan syndrome: Fibrillin is a family of proteins related to the scaffolding necessary for the deposition of elastin. Mutations in the fibrillin gene result in Marfan syndrome, a disease characterized by a lack of resistance in the tissues rich in elastic fibers. Because the large arteries are rich in components of the elastic system and because the blood pressure is high in the aorta, patients with this disease often experience aortic rupture, a life-threatening condition.

  • Edema is promoted by the accumulation of water in the extracellular spaces. Water in the extracellular compartment of connective tissue comes from the blood, passing through the capillary walls into the extracellular compartment of the tissue. The capillary wall is only slightly permeable to macromolecules but permits the passage of water and small molecules, including low-molecular-weight proteins.

  • Obesity: It results from an excessive accumulation of fat in unilocular tissue cells that have become larger than usual (hypertrophic obesity). An increase in the number of adipocytes causes hyperplastic obesity.

  • Lipomas and liposarcomas: Unilocular adipocytes can generate very common benign tumors called lipomas. Malignant adipocyte-derived tumors (liposarcomas) are not frequent in humans.
