INTRODUCTION

Epithelium is one of the primary tissues, which is formed by the closely aggregated cells with minimal intercellular substance (cell adhesive molecules—CAM) between them. In addition to CAM, the epithelial cells exhibit specializations or junctional complexes. Epithelium is a sheet of cells that covers the external surface of any solid structure and the internal surface of any hollow or tubular structure or that forms the lining of the body surface, passages and cavities.

Epithelium is bound to subepithelial tissue by a basement membrane which is made up of (a) basal lamina (product of epithelium) and (b) reticular lamina (product of connective tissue). Epithelium is avascular (no blood vessels or lymphatics) and nourished by tissue fluid. It invaginates to form glands.

Epithelium is classified on the basis of (i) the number of cell layers present, (ii) the shape of the cells, (iii) special features on the surface of the cells, e.g., glycocalyx (cell coat or fuzzy coat), microvilli (brush or striated border), stereocilia and cilia.

Simple Squamous Epithelium

Cells are thin, flat and plate-like. Nucleus is oval or round, central and forms a bulge in the contour of the cell.

For example—endothelium, mesothelium, alveoli of lung, parietal layer of Bowman's capsule, loop of Henle, etc.

Simple Cuboidal Epithelium

All dimensions of the cells are equal. Nucleus is round and central.

For example—thyroid follicles, smallest ducts of glands, germinal epithelium of ovary, tubules of kidney, pigmented layer of retina, etc.8

Simple Columnar Epithelium

Cell height is more than the rest of dimensions. Nucleus is oval or elongated and basal. It is of two types:

  1. Non-ciliated, e.g., Stomach, intestine gallbladder, larger ducts of glands, etc.

  2. Ciliated: Free surface of cells bears cilia, e.g., Parts of uterus and fallopian tube, terminal bronchioles, central canal of spinal cord, etc.

Pseudostratified Epithelium

Cells are columnar but of varying heights and so nuclei are seen at varying levels giving a false appearance of multilayers of cells. Two types: (i) Pseudostratified columnar non-ciliated epithelium, e.g., Larger ducts of salivary glands, parts of urethra, parts of anal canal, etc. (ii) Pseudostratified columnar ciliated epithelium, e.g., Nasal cavity, trachea, bronchi, epididymis and vas deferens (stereocilia), etc.

Stratified Epithelium

Stratified epithelium has more than one layer of cells. It is mainly of four types:

  1. Stratified squamous epithelium: The deepest layer has columnar cells, the intermediate layers have polyhedral cells and surface layers have flattened cells.

    Two subtypes

    1. Stratified squamous non-keratinized epithelium: No keratin layer on the surface, e.g., Mouth cavity, pharynx, esophagus, lower part of anal canal, vagina, cornea, etc. and

    2. Stratified squamous keratinized epithelium: Surface cells are dead, enucleated, desquamated and present a fibrillar strands called keratin, e.g., Epidermis

  2. Stratified cuboidal epithelium: Usually several layers of cells, of which the superficial ones are cuboidal and the rest is polyhedral, e.g., ovarian follicles, duct of sweat gland, interlobar ducts of salivary glands, etc.

  3. Stratified columnar epithelium: It is rare type and forms a transition between different types of epithelium like between pseudostratified and stratified or simple and stratified. The deeper layers have polyhedral cells while superficial layer has columnar cells, e.g., Transitional zones of epithelium in anal canal and pharynx, palpebral conjunctiva, ducts of pancreas and mammary glands, etc.

  4. Transitional epithelium: This is misnomer term because it is not a transition between two types of epithelium. It has the property of being stretched considerably. In unstretched condition it has 5–6 layers. The basal cells are columnar, the intermediate layers contain pear-shaped or polyhedral cells and the surface cells are large usually umbrella-shaped, sometimes binucleated. This epithelium is also called urothelium, as it forms an effective barrier to urine, e.g., Pelvis of kidney, ureter, urinary bladder and proximal part of urethra.9

Clinical Correlation/Vertical Integration

I. Metaplasia

Under certain abnormal conditions, one type of epithelial tissue may undergo transformation into another type. This reversible process is called metaplasia (Gr. metaplasis, transformation).

Examples

  • Barrett's esophagus: In patients of chronic gastroesophageal reflux, stratified squamous non-keratinized epithelium lining the lower esophagus is replaced by columnar epithelium. This condition is known as Barrett's esophagus.

  • In heavy cigarette smokers, the ciliated pseudostratified epithelium lining the bronchi can be transformed into stratified squamous epithelium.

  • In individuals with chronic vitamin A deficiency, epithelial tissues of the type found in the bronchi and urinary bladder are gradually replaced by stratified squamous epithelium.

  • Squamous metaplasia of cervix: Low pH of vagina stimulates the transformation of mucus secreting columnar epithelium/glandular epithelium to stratified squamous non-keratinized epithelium. It is a benign condition.

Metaplasia is not restricted to epithelial tissue; it also occurs in connective tissue.

II. Epithelial Cell-derived Tumors

Both benign and malignant tumors can arise from most types of epithelial cells.

In children up to the age of 10 years, most tumors develop (in decreasing order) from hematopoietic organs, nerve tissues, connective tissues, and epithelial tissues. This proportion gradually changes, and after age 45 years, more than 90% of all tumors that develop are of epithelial origin.

A carcinoma (Gr. karkinos, crab, + oma, tumor) is a malignant tumor of epithelial cell origin.

Examples

  • Benign tumors: Papilloma, acanthoma, keratoacanthoma, nevi.

  • Malignant tumors: Squamous cell carcinoma, basal cell carcinoma, adenocarcinoma.
