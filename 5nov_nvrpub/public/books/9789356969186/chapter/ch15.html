INTRODUCTION

It consists of internal and external sex organs.

  • The internal sex organs include a pair of ovaries, a pair of uterine or fallopian tubes, uterus and vagina.

  • The external sex organs include external genitalia and mammary gland.

  • The mammary gland, placenta and umbilical cord are included in female reproductive system because of their direct relations.

OVARY

Microscopically each ovary is covered by a simple cuboidal epithelium called germinal epithelium, but it is a misnomer as it does not give rise to germ cells or oocytes.

  • Deep to germinal epithelium is a layer of dense connective tissue called tunica albuginea. Ovary shows a peripheral cortex and a central medulla.

  • The cortex contains ovarian follicles at different stages of maturation and degeneration. It may also contain corpus luteum and corpus albicans.

  • The follicles are surrounded by highly cellular connective tissue stroma containing numerous fibroblasts.

  • The medulla contains loose fibroelastic connective tissue containing blood vessels, lymphatics and nerves.

At birth about 4,00,000 oocytes are present in the ovaries. Most of them degenerate before puberty and only about 40,000 oocytes remain. Of these only about 400 oocytes undergo ovulation during reproductive period (13–15 years to 45–55 years) of about 30–40 years of a female. The rest undergo degeneration/atresia.151

The follicular growth and maturation is called folliculogenesis.

It has five stages:

  1. Primordial follicle: Smallest and located at the periphery of cortex. It consists of primary oocytes (about 25 um) surrounded by a single layer of squamous follicular cells

  2. Primary follicle: The primary oocyte enlarges (40–80 μm) and surrounding squamous cells become cuboidal. A glycoprotein layer called zona pellucida is formed between oocyte and surrounding follicular cells

  3. Secondary follicle: The follicular cells proliferate to form several layers and cells are now called granulosa cells. The connective tissue begins to condense outside the follicle to form theca folliculi

  4. Antral follicle: The primary oocyte again enlarges to its full size (about 125 μm) and irregular fluid filled spaces appear among the surrounding granulosa cells which join to form a crescentic space or antrum filled with liquor folliculi. The theca folliculi differentiate into an inner cellular theca interna and an outer fibrous theca externa

  5. Mature/Graafian follicle: The follicle enlarges in size and antrum divides the granulosa cells into two sets, the outer set which lines the antrum called membrana granulosa and the inner set which surrounds the oocyte is called cumulus oophorus.

The cells which attach oocyte to the wall are called discus proligerus. At this stage primary oocyte completes its 1st meiotic division and becomes secondary oocyte/ovum.

The size of follicle becomes about 10–15 mm and is seen bulging out on the surface of the ovary. At mid-menstrual cycle (about 14th day of 28 days menstrual cycle) under the influence of hormones there is sudden rise in the quantity of liquor folliculi which causes rupture of mature follicle and release of ovum with one layer of adherent granulosa cells (corona radiata) in to the peritoneal cavity

This process is called ovulation. Then released ovum is drawn into the uterine tube.

Atretic follicles: Several follicles start maturing during each ovarian or menstrual cycle but only one attains full maturity and undergo ovulation. The other follicles degenerate and become atretic follicles like scars.

Corpus luteum: After ovulation the wall of the follicle collapses and gets transformed into a temporary endocrine organ called corpus luteum. It consists of two types of cells

  1. Lutein cells which are numerous, larger, with pale cytoplasm and derived from granulosa cells. They secrete hormone progesterone

  2. Paralutein cells which are less numerous, smaller, with dark cytoplasm and derived from theca interna cells. They secrete hormone estrogen. If the liberated ovum is fertilized the corpus luteum survives for next 4–5 months and is called corpus luteum of pregnancy but if fertilization does not take place the corpus luteum survives for only 10–12 days and is called corpus luteum of menstruation. In both cases after functional activity is over the corpus luteum undergoes regression and is replaced by a fibrous scar called the corpus albicans (white body).

UTERINE TUBE (FALLOPIAN TUBE)

Its wall presents three coats from within outside

  1. Mucous coat: lining epithelium is simple columnar ciliated having two types of cells ciliated columnar and non-ciliated peg (secretory) cells. This coat presents branching folds making the lumen irregular

  2. Muscular coat: consists of smooth muscle fibers arranged in inner circular and outer longitudinal layers

  3. Serous coat: consists of mesothelium (peritoneum) supported by connective tissue.

UTERUS

Its wall presents three coats from within outside

  1. Endometrium: It is the mucosal lining consisting of simple columnar partly ciliated epithelium and underlying lamina propria rich in cells and no collagen fibers.

  2. The lamina propria also contains many blood vessels and simple tubular uterine glands

  3. The endometrium is normally divisible into two layers, the superficial stratum functionalis (2/3rd) the deep stratum basalis (1/3rd)152

  4. The arteries in the endometrium are also of two types, straight in stratum basalis and coiled or spiral in stratum functionalis.

  5. Stratum functionalis exhibits cyclic structural changes in response to ovarian hormones estrogen and progesterone during phases of menstrual or ovarian cycle.

  6. These changes involve only fundus and body of uterus and not cervix.

  7. In menstrual phase there is shedding of endometrium until only raw surface of stratum basalis is left.

  • The endometrium of cervix does not undergo cyclic changes during menstrual cycle. It contains large branched cervical glands which shows altered secretory activity in proliferative phase (thin and watery) and secretory phase (thick and viscus).

  • This altered secretion is important for the passage of sperms. No spiral arteries. Myometrium is about 15 mm thick, smooth muscle fibers arranged in 3 ill-defined layers, inner and outer longitudinal; and middle circular, highly vascular, so called stratum vasculare.

  • During pregnancy, muscle fibers undergo hyperplasia and hypertrophy.

  • Perimetrium is serous coat presenting mesothelial lining supported by connective tissue rich in elastic fibers and blood vessels.

(Fibroid uterus or uterine leiomyoma is common benign tumor presenting nodules/whorls of smooth muscle within myometrium. Cancer of cervix or cervical carcinoma arises from epithelium of intravaginal part of cervix.)

Vagina

It is about 8 cm long and capable of considerable elongation and distension. Its wall presents three coats:

  1. Mucous coat presents numerous folds, lining epithelium thick stratified squamous non-keratinized may show partial keratinization, under influence of estrogen epithelium synthesizes and accumulates glycogen, lamina propria rich in elastic fibers, highly vascular with many veins

  2. Muscular coat presents smooth muscle fibers arranged in thick outer longitudinal and thin inner circular layers. At lower end striated muscle fibers form sphincter (bulbospongiosus muscle)

  3. Adventitial coat presents dense connective tissue containing elastic fibers, blood vessels, nerve fibers and lymphatics.

Cervix

  1. The cervical canal is lined with tall, mucus-secreting columnar epithelium.

  2. Similar epithelium also lines the numerous highly branched and tubular cervical glands which extend deep into the wide lamina propria.

  3. The connective tissue in the lamina propria of the cervix is more fibrous than in the uterus with blood vessels, nerves, and occasional lymphatic nodules.

  4. The columnar epithelium of the cervical canal at the lower end abruptly changes to non-keratinized stratified squamous epithelium.153

UMBILICAL CORD

It is cross-section at term shows:

  1. A single umbilical vein (thin wall with wide lumen) bringing oxygenated blood from the placenta to the fetus

  2. Two umbilical arteries (thick wall with narrow lumen) carrying deoxygenated blood from the fetus to the placenta

  3. A mass of gelatinous mucoid connective tissue (Wharton's jelly) in which these vessels are embedded and

  4. A tube of amnion (simple cuboidal epithelium) ensheathing all the above structures.

  5. Cross-section of umbilical cord shows a single-layered amnion lined with cuboidal epithelium.

  6. It is filled with a mucoid connective tissue (Wharton's jelly) comprising of network of fibrocytes and spindle-like mesenchymal cells supported by collagen fibers.

  7. Single umbilical vein and two umbilical arteries are embedded in the connective tissue.

  8. The umbilical arteries are muscular, medium-sized arteries while the umbilical vein has a thick circular muscle layer in contrast to thick longitudinal muscle layer seen in adult veins.

PLACENTA

It is a specialized extraembryonic tissue in which blood vessels of fetus are brought in close intimacy with the maternal blood for nutrition, respiration, excretion and other metabolic activities during intrauterine life. It is discoidal in shape at term, measures 15–20 cm in diameter and 3 cm in thickness; weighs about 500 g. It is derived from fetal (chorion) and maternal (decidua basalis) components. Fetal surface is smooth and shiny covered with amnion and maternal surface is rough sending incomplete septae towards fetal surface and presents 15–20 cotyledons.

A section shows many villi (containing fetal blood in capillaries) separated by intervillous space (containing maternal blood). Exchange of gases and metabolites take place at placental barrier. Each villus has a central core of connective tissue containing branches of umbilical vessels, inner layer of cytotrophoblast (disappears in second half of pregnancy), outer layer of syncytiotrophoblast, which produces progesterone, estrogen, human chorionic gonadotropin (hCG) and other placental hormones.155

  • Placenta is lined internally (fetal side) by the amniotic membrane and chorionic plate and externally (maternal side) by the basal plate

  • The intervillous space containing the stem villi with their branches, is filled with maternal blood

  • The surface of the amnion is lined by the squamous epithelium.

  • Below the connective tissue layer is the trophoblast of the chorion

  • The trophoblast and the underlying connective tissue form the chorionic plate.

Clinical Correlation/Vertical Integration

  • In cases of abnormal nidation, the embryo may implant itself in the oviduct (ectopic pregnancy). In this case, the lamina propria reacts like the endometrium, forming numerous decidual cells. Because of its small diameter, the oviduct cannot contain the growing embryo and bursts, causing extensive hemorrhage that can be fatal if not treated immediately.

  • The initial attachment of the embryo usually occurs on the ventral or dorsal walls of the corpus of the uterus. Sometimes the embryo attaches close to the internal os. In this case the placenta will be interposed between the fetus and the vagina, obstructing the passage of the fetus at parturition. This situation, called placenta previa, must be recognized by the physician, and the fetus must be delivered by cesarean section; otherwise, the fetus may die. Sometimes, as already mentioned, the embryo attaches to the epithelium of the uterine tube. Very rarely the zygote may enter the abdominal cavity, attach to the peritoneum, and develop there.

  • Cancer of the cervix (cervical carcinoma) is derived from its stratified squamous epithelium. Although it is frequently observed, the mortality rate is low (8 per 100,000). This low rate is due to the usual discovery of the carcinoma in its early stages, made possible by yearly physical observation of the cervix and by cytological analysis of smears of the cervical epithelium (Papanicolaou test).

  • Exfoliative cytology is the study of the characteristics of cells that normally desquamate from various surfaces of the body. Cytological examination of cells collected from the vagina provides information of clinical importance.

  • In fully mature vaginal mucosa, five types of cells are easily identified: cells of the internal portion of the basal layer (basal cells), cells of the external portion of the basal layer (parabasal cells), cells of the intermediate layers, precornified cells, and cornified cells. Based on the numbers of cell types that appear in a vaginal smear, valuable information can be obtained on the hormonal status of the patient (action of estrogen and progesterone). The vaginal smear is also useful in the early detection of cervical cancer.

  • The first secretion from the mammary glands to appear after birth is called colostrum. It contains less fat and more protein than regular milk and is rich in antibodies (predominantly secretory IgA) that provide some degree of passive immunity to the newborn, especially within the gut lumen.

  • When a woman is breast-feeding, the nursing action of the child stimulates tactile receptors in the nipple, resulting in liberation of the posterior pituitary hormone oxytocin. This hormone causes contraction of myoepithelial cells in alveoli and ducts, resulting in ejection of milk (milk-ejection reflex). Negative emotional stimuli, such as frustration, anxiety, or anger, can inhibit the liberation of oxytocin and thus prevent the reflex.

  • About 9% of all women born in the United States will develop breast cancer at some time during their lives. Most of these cancers (carcinomas) arise from epithelial cells of the lactiferous ducts. If these cells metastasize to the lungs, brain, or bone, breast carcinoma becomes a major cause of death. Early detection (e.g., through self-examination, mammography, ultrasound, and other techniques) and consequent early treatment have significantly reduced the mortality rate from breast cancer.
