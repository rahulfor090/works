7MADHAW DAS BIJLANI: A TRIBUTE

Full many a flower is born to blush unseen

And waste its sweetness on desert air

— THOMAS GRAY

Shri Madhaw Das Bijlani was a self-made man who did not know when he was born, and did not care about it. When he was barely five, his father passed away leaving behind an illiterate widow, and three children – Madhaw, his elder sister and his younger brother. Forced by circumstances to leave school and start earning when he was still in his early teens, he continued to learn, initially with the help of a Gandhian cousin, and then on his own. The result was that without having even a school leaving certificate, he could have easily beaten an average graduate and many postgraduates so far as command over the English language goes. He never really stopped learning. He read slowly but thoroughly, made sure that he understood every word – may be with the help of a dictionary, and appreciated an elegant expression, going over it again and again to enjoy its beauty and charm. His handwriting was almost like calligraphy, and his grammar was near-perfect. His enterprising nature made it difficult for him to stick to one business for very long, and through sheer hard work, he made a success of whatever he decided to do. Some of the most cherished years of his life were spent as a medical representative with Unichem, and later as a manufacturing chemist in Karachi. This brought him in contact with doctors, and created in him a strong ambition that his children should be doctors. God helps those who help themselves: both his sons became doctors, and got married to doctors, with the result that he became the proud father of four doctors.

Age had dulled neither his inquisitiveness nor his sensitivity, and I wonder how much of it was due to lack of formal education. He retained a child-like simplicity, curiosity and sense of wonder till the very end of his life. He had a great talent for innovation and improvisation. Had his 8circumstances been more favourable, he could have become a great scientist. His qualities of the heart were no less remarkable than those of the head. Ever considerate to the less fortunate, he had a knack for spotting people he could possibly help; even those whom he scarcely knew. During his morning and evening walks he would help a blind person cross the road, or a labourer push his load.

He had a philosophical bent of mind, and cherished some perennial values such as truth, honesty and dignity of labour. He was ethical without being ritualistic, was above caste and creed, and was sensitive to injustice and falsehood. Age had not immunized him to the ills of the society – he could not accept with equanimity corruption, neglect of duty, environmental pollution, sex and violence on the TV, and chaotic traffic.

He voluntarily lived a life much simpler than he could afford, and enjoyed it. He ate simple wholesome food, took regular exercise, and looked after his body well. Till his last day, he walked erect, and nobody could have anticipated his end. Possibly as a reward for the way he had lived, God gave him a swift and painless death on 4 November 1996 at the age of about 75. The last thing that he did was to donate his eyes, so that somebody would be able to see once again. He left for his heavenly abode with a sense of fulfillment, and without the burden of unfinished responsibilities. The way he left us reminds me of what Charlotte Bronte said about her sister Emily: “Never in all her life had she lingered over any task that lay before her, and she did not linger now. She sank rapidly. She made haste to leave us”.9

11FOREWORD

Sukham samagram vijnane vimala ca pratishthitham

(All happiness is rooted in good science)

— CHARAKA

Research provides a means to enhance one's knowledge, to broaden one's intellectual horizon, to satisfy one's curiosity, to provide objective basis for one's professional activity, to develop new techniques and technologies to solve unresolved problems, to encourage self-reliance and confidence, and on a more philosophical level a path to search for truth.

Research promotes a sense of enquiry and scientific temper, inculcates a discipline for scientific rigor, encourages objectivity and humility, fosters self confidence and can be a source of immense happiness. Millennia ago Socrates exhorted, “To live on the ideas of others is a sickness”. And nearer home the angel saint of Indian Science, Shri Jawaharlal Nehru, reminded us, “Always we must hold to our anchor of precise knowledge, tested by reason and even more by experiments and practice”.

The second half of the twentieth century made revolutionary changes in the practice of medicine not only through introduction of advances in new techniques and technologies but even more so by emphasis on evidence based medicine, thus highlighting the need for research.

It is generally recognized that India has made creditable progress in the practice of medicine but it has miserably lagged behind in the field of biomedical research. At the same time there are numerous problems that bedevil the health of the nation, for which we alone have to find solutions; others are obviously not interested in them. An estimated 93 percent of World's burden of preventable mortality (measured in years of potential life lost) occurs in the developing world. Today less than 10 percent of global research and development budget is used to address 12the largest disease burden, which is found among the poorer populations of the World.

Burdened with a heavy load of clinical work, not provided with basic training in research methodology, paucity of adequate infrastructure and scientific environment and consequent lack of mentorship for research, the teachers in our medical colleges are often blamed for our poor scientific contribution.

Prof. Bijlani deserves our gratitude for producing this comprehensive book on Medical Research to address the existing lacunae in our medical education. He has provided a systematic account of the various steps involved in medical research and made this interesting by simple, practical examples, questions and answers, illustrative cartoons, thought promoting quotations and relevant references for further reading.

Though modestly recommended by him as a work for beginners, I must confess that it had valuable information even for individuals like me who have devoted a lifetime to practice and promotion of biomedical research. I strongly recommend that it should be made a compulsory part of the curriculum, if not for undergraduates, at least for postgraduate students.

BEFORE YOU READ THE BOOK … …

Medical doctors in India join postgraduate courses with little more than a vague idea about the method of going about research. During the three years that they spend as postgraduates, they do some research for their thesis, and in the process pick up also something about the process of research. That, in a way, is the best way to learn research methodology – just as the best way to learn swimming is to get thrown into the water. However, some theoretical knowledge may encourage the beginner to jump into the water and also avoid some fatal mistakes.

If the only aim of this book were to guide postgraduate students in their thesis work, one-third of the book may not be necessary. However, this apparent redundancy also has a two-fold purpose. Firstly, although most of the work involved in research is sheer drudgery, there is in it also a romantic element – enough of it to serve as compensation and justification for all the drudgery. The book is an introduction not just to the mechanical and repetitive aspects of research but also to its romantic side. Secondly, it is a sad truth that the quality of medical research in India leaves much to be desired. This book is designed to make a humble contribution towards improving the quality of our research by inspiring the readers to select better questions for research and to go about addressing the questions in the best possible way. Improving the quality of research is more a question of attitude than hard work: the amount of work required for doing mediocre research is exactly the same as that for the best of research. It is hoped that the ‘adiposity’ of the book will enlighten realism with romance, and immunize the reader against the temptations of mediocrity.
