BIOFEEDBACK AS A CLINICAL TOOL

The history of biofeedback reflects a journey from ancient practices to cutting-edge technological interventions. Its evolution is marked by contributions from physiology, psychology, and technology, making it an indispensable tool in modern therapeutic and self-regulation practices. As biofeedback continues to integrate with emerging technologies, its potential for enhancing health and well-being will likely expand further.

Ancient and prescientific concepts: Early forms of biofeedback were implicit in practices like yoga, meditation, and mindfulness, which focused on gaining control over bodily processes. Traditional practices often emphasized breathing, heart rate, and mental focus, laying the groundwork for modern biofeedback principles.

Early physiological discoveries: In the 19th century, advances in physiology and the invention of instruments like the sphygmograph (for measuring pulse) and galvanometer (for electrical activity) allowed for the study of bodily functions. Pioneers such as Claude Bernard and Ivan Pavlov explored the body's internal regulation and response systems, providing a scientific basis for biofeedback.276

1940s–1950s: Laying the scientific foundation: Norbert Wiener (Cybernetics, 1948): The concept of feedback loops in cybernetics influenced the understanding of how individuals could regulate physiological functions. Wilder Penfield: Research on the brain and neural pathways demonstrated that physiological responses could be monitored and influenced. Early experiments began to show the possibility of voluntary control over autonomic processes.

1960s: Emergence of biofeedback as a field: Neal Miller (1960): Demonstrated that autonomic nervous system responses, previously thought to be involuntary, could be conditioned. His experiments with rats showed that heart rate, blood pressure, and other autonomic functions could be modified using feedback. Joseph Kamiya (1962): Conducted groundbreaking work on alpha wave training using electroencephalography (EEG). His studies revealed that individuals could be trained to control brain wave patterns, marking the beginning of biofeedback in neuroscience. Barbara Brown: Popularized biofeedback through her research and writings, focusing on the psychological aspects and public education.

1970s: Growth of clinical applications: Biofeedback was recognized as a therapeutic tool for stress, anxiety, and pain management. Organizations like the Biofeedback Research Society (founded in 1969) (later renamed the Association for Applied Psychophysiology and Biofeedback) played a key role in advancing the field. Clinical studies explored its use in treating migraines, hypertension, and muscle rehabilitation. Popular devices included the electromyography (EMG), galvanic skin response (GSR) and EEG machines.

1980s: Technological advancements and refinements: Integration with technology: Advances in computing and electronics led to more sophisticated and portable biofeedback devices. Techniques such as heart rate variability (HRV) training and thermal biofeedback were developed for broader clinical applications.

1990s: Evidence-based practice: A growing body of research validated the effectiveness of biofeedback for conditions like chronic pain, tension headaches, and urinary incontinence. Integration of biofeedback into physical therapy and rehabilitation became common. Functional MRI (fMRI) and real-time imaging provided new insights into the brain's role in biofeedback.

2000s–present: Contemporary biofeedback: Expansion and digitalization: Development of wearable devices and apps for stress and sleep monitoring has made biofeedback more accessible to the general population. Neurofeedback, a subtype of biofeedback focusing on brain wave control, gained popularity for conditions like ADHD, PTSD, and epilepsy. Virtual reality (VR) and artificial intelligence (AI) began to integrate with biofeedback, creating immersive and adaptive training experiences. The focus shifted to personalized medicine, emphasizing individualized protocols and real-time monitoring.277

Since the 1960s, biofeedback has been recognized as a clinical tool in clinical medicine, behavioral medicine, physiotherapy and rehabilitation medicine. Information regarding history of biofeedback is not readily available to physiotherapists and it does not appear to be widely described or discussed. Various references suggest that the systematic study of biofeedback started in 1960s with attempts to train voluntary control of autonomic function, such as heart rate and blood pressure. Although significant improvement may occur in different conditions, how far the biofeedback devices are effective, should be verified by repeated research in this field. In general, biofeedback can be defined as the process of furnishing an individual with information about body functioning, so as to get some voluntary control over it (Fig. 12.1). This information regarding body functioning can be given to the individual via visual or auditory signal through a suitable instrument.

TYPES OF BIOFEEDBACK

The various forms of biofeedback devices are as follows:

Myoelectric Feedback

Myoelectric feedback is also known as EMG biofeedback and is commonly used in physiotherapy (Fig. 12.2). A set of surface electrodes is placed on the skin over a chosen muscle or muscle group to detect electrical signals associated with muscle contraction.278

Fig. 12.2 Myoelectric biofeedback device.

These signals are amplified and translated into simple auditory and visual signals that are very easy to understand. This auditory or visual information concerning the state of muscle tension or relaxation is provided to the patient via auditory or visual display. Auditory display is in the form of clicks or buzzing sound and visual display is in the form of movement of meter needle or glowing of the lights or computer display, rather than an oscilloscope trace. As this display bears an approximate relationship to the magnitude of the muscle contraction causing it, re-education of the muscles is possible. In other words, it is possible to get the desired response from the muscles with this device by increasing or decreasing the activity of these muscles. Myoelectric biofeedback systems include surface electrodes attached to the skin over the muscle group being monitored to detect electrical activity. Amplifiers magnify the weak EMG signals for processing. Processing units convert the raw EMG signals into meaningful data or graphical representations. Feedback mechanisms provide real-time feedback via a computer screen, auditory tones, or other output devices.

Applications

The applications of myoelectric biofeedback include:

  • Muscle rehabilitation: Helps regain strength and control in weakened or injured muscles.279

  • Postsurgical rehabilitation: Facilitates recovery after orthopedic or neurological surgeries by encouraging muscle activation, training specific muscle activity after a tendon or muscle transplant, and dystonic conditions.

  • Stroke rehabilitation: Assists in regaining voluntary motor control in paralyzed or spastic muscles, peripheral nerve injuries, writer's cramp, blepharospasm.

  • Chronic pain management: Reduces muscle tension associated with conditions like low back pain or fibromyalgia.

  • Tension headaches: Teaches relaxation of hyperactive neck and scalp muscles. It is also used to improve shoulder control, to reeducate dorsiflexion of foot and to reduce spasticity of plantar flexors in hemiplegia. Biofeedback devices can also be used in the treatment of spasticity in cerebral palsy and multiple sclerosis.

  • Cerebral palsy: Encourages improved motor control and muscle coordination in children. Multiple sclerosis: Helps manage spasticity and optimize muscle use.

  • Stress and anxiety management: Reduces muscle tension and promotes relaxation in individuals with stress-related disorders.

  • Orthopedic conditions: Addresses conditions like patellofemoral pain syndrome, rotator cuff injuries, and temporomandibular joint (TMJ) dysfunction by improving muscle balance.

  • Sports performance: Enhances athletic performance by improving motor control, strength, and coordination. Assists in correcting improper movement patterns.

  • Pelvic floor dysfunction: Used in managing conditions like urinary incontinence, pelvic pain, or postpartum muscle weakness.

Advantages

The advantages of myoelectric biofeedback include that it is non-invasive, and it involves no surgical procedures or medication, making it a safe option for most patients. It provides real-time feedback and immediate insights into muscle activity, allowing for precise adjustments. It offers personalized and tailored programs to individual needs and specific muscle groups. It is versatile and useful in a wide range of medical, therapeutic, and performance contexts.

Postural Biofeedback

Postural biofeedback is a therapeutic approach used to help individuals become more aware of their posture and correct it through real-time feedback. This technique is commonly used in physiotherapy, ergonomics, and rehabilitation to address postural abnormalities, prevent injuries, and enhance overall musculoskeletal health.280

Components of postural biofeedback are:

  • Sensors and devices: Wearable sensors (e.g., accelerometers, gyroscopes, and strain gauges) detect postural deviations. Electronic devices or apps provide visual, auditory, or tactile feedback to the user.

  • Feedback modalities:

    • Visual feedback: Mirrors, screen displays, or augmented reality tools showing real-time postural alignment.

    • Auditory feedback: Alerts or tones signaling incorrect posture.

    • Tactile feedback: Vibrations or gentle physical prompts delivered via wearables.

  • Software Integration: Applications and platforms analyze postural patterns, track progress, and offer corrective exercises.

Applications of postural biofeedback are:

  • Rehabilitation for postural correction for patients with conditions like scoliosis, kyphosis, or lordosis and recovery from musculoskeletal injuries such as back pain or neck strain.

  • Ergonomic training of workers to maintain proper posture during desk work or manual tasks to prevent repetitive strain injuries.

  • Assisting athletes in their sports performance in achieving optimal body mechanics and reducing injury risk.

  • Managing postural problems associated with chronic conditions like Parkinson's disease, cerebral palsy, or stroke.

  • Daily life improvement by enhancing awareness in individuals with poor postural habits due to sedentary lifestyles.

Benefits of postural biofeedback are that it improves postural alignment and reduces strain on muscles and joints, prevents chronic pain and injuries, enhances body awareness and proprioception and supports long-term behavioral change through consistent practice. Limitations of the postural biofeedback are it requires consistent effort and motivation from the user, some devices can be expensive or may not fit all body types and an incorrect usage or over-reliance on feedback tools may hinder natural motor learning.

Feedback Goniometers

Feedback goniometer devices monitor the joint angles. An elbow angle monitor is used to increase the range of motion of elbow joints. Knee angle monitor is useful in prevention of hyperextension at knee. Ankle angle monitors can be used in the treatment of footdrop during swing phase of the gait. Hip rotation monitor device measures internal and external rotation of the foot with respect to the pelvis and may be useful in correction of gait deviations such as, excessive internal or external rotation of hip.281

Feedback goniometers are advanced tools used in physiotherapy, rehabilitation, and research to measure joint angles and provide real-time feedback for corrective actions. Unlike traditional goniometers, feedback goniometers incorporate sensors and electronics to enhance accuracy and user engagement.

Key features of feedback goniometers are:

  • Electronic sensors: Use accelerometers, gyroscopes, or potentiometers to measure angular displacement. Capture dynamic motion and static joint positions.

  • Real-time feedback: Provide auditory, visual, or tactile feedback to guide the user toward proper joint alignment or range of motion (ROM).

  • Data integration: Sync with apps, monitors, or computers for detailed analysis. Store and display data for progress tracking and evaluation.

  • Customizable settings: Adjust thresholds for feedback based on patient needs or therapeutic goals. Include different modes for specific activities, such as gait training or stretching exercises.

Applications of feedback goniometers are:

  • They assess and improve ROM in conditions like arthritis, post-surgical recovery, or frozen shoulder and it can monitor patient progress in therapy sessions.

  • In rehabilitation, it trains patients in correct joint positioning for exercises, it can prevent compensatory movements that could hinder recovery.

  • It can also be useful in ergonomics and work safety since it evaluates and optimize joint angles during repetitive tasks and alert users when they exceed safe joint limits.

  • It can also be helpful in sports performance and training as it can help athletes maintain proper biomechanics during activities and analyze performance metrics and identify areas for improvement.

  • They are also used in research to collect precise movement data for studies on joint mechanics, gait analysis, or musculoskeletal disorders.

Benefits of feedback goniometers are that they provide objective and accurate joint angle measurements, enhances patient engagement with real-time corrections, reduces the risk of injuries by ensuring proper joint alignment and it facilitates detailed documentation for progress tracking. Popular feedback goniometers are wearable feedback goniometers, handheld electronic goniometers and app based integrated systems.282

Various challenges and limitations of feedback goniometers are that they are very much costly as compared to traditional goniometers. These devices require proper calibration and handling for accurate results, and it may be difficult to use with individuals who have severe mobility restrictions like individuals having ankylosing spondylitis.

Pressure or Force Biofeedback

Pressure or force biofeedback devices monitor the pressure or force. The limb load monitor consists of a shoe insole with a pressure sensor, which senses the amount of the weight applied to it. It is used to encourage patients like hemiplegics to put more weight on the affected leg while standing or walking. A prosthetic grip strength monitor is applied to the myoelectric upper limb prosthesis to strengthen of the grip.

Popular devices for pressure or force biofeedback are:

  • Stabilizer pressure biofeedback unit (Chattanooga): Measures lumbar and cervical spine stability during core exercises.

  • Biodex force plates: Used for balance and gait analysis.

  • Perineometers: Used to train pelvic floor muscle strength and endurance.

  • Hand-held dynamometers: Used to assess and train muscle strength in limbs.

  • Tekscan pressure mapping systems: Provide detailed pressure distribution for gait and posture analysis.

Pressure or force biofeedback is a type of biofeedback used in physiotherapy to monitor and train muscle activity, joint stability, and motor control by measuring the amount of force or pressure exerted by the body. This real-time feedback helps patients develop better awareness and control of their movements, making it particularly useful for rehabilitation and performance enhancement. These pressure or force biofeedback devices through sensors measure pressure or force applied during movements or exercises. Data is displayed in real time on a screen or device as graphs, numbers, or visual cues. Patients receive feedback based on their ability to maintain or adjust pressure/force within a target range. Patients practice exercises, adjusting their force or pressure output to achieve the desired results. Therapists use this information to guide technique corrections and progression. Inflatable pressure biofeedback units (e.g., stabilizer by Chattanooga) are used for core stability and spinal rehabilitation and it can assess and train deep core muscles (e.g., transversus abdominis) for spinal stability during exercises for low back pain patients as well as it can assess deep neck flexors in the treatment of neck pain.283

In pelvic floor rehabilitation, perineometers or pressure sensors are used to train pelvic floor muscles to address incontinence, pelvic organ prolapse, or postnatal recovery. For gait and balance training, force plates or pressure-sensitive insoles to improve weight distribution, balance, and gait mechanics in patients with neurological or orthopedic conditions.

In postsurgical rehabilitation, force sensors or dynamometers are used to guide progressive loading during rehabilitation for joint replacements or ligament repairs.

In upper limb rehabilitation, hand-held dynamometers or grip strength sensors are used to assess and train strength and coordination in patients with neurological injuries or orthopedic conditions. In sports, force plates or load sensors are used to optimize force production, technique, and injury prevention strategies in athletes and in pain management pressure or force sensors integrated into therapeutic setups to teach proper load distribution and reduce compensatory movements causing pain.

Advantages of pressure or force biofeedback are that they provide objective accurate, measurable data to guide therapy and track progress. It improves motor learning through real-time feedback enhances awareness of proper movement patterns. They can be customized and adapted for various conditions and levels of physical ability. These devices enhance the patient engagement through interactive feedback motivates patients to actively participate in therapy. These pressure or force biofeedback devices can prevent overloading and help to ensure patients use appropriate force or pressure during exercises, reducing the risk of re-injury. These biofeedback devices supports the functional rehabilitation as they can integrate dynamic tasks, improving carryover into daily activities.

However, the disadvantages are that this equipment specially force plates can be expensive, they require skill and therapist need training to set up, interpret, and guide patients effectively. There is a limited accessibility since not all clinics may have access to specialized devices. Patients may become dependent and there is potential risk of overreliance on visual feedback and struggle to replicate results without it and external environmental sensitivity due to factors such as unstable surfaces or patient variability, can affect readings.284

Orofacial Control Devices

Orofacial control devices can be used in the treatment of orofacial problems in cerebral palsy children. Jaw closure monitor is helpful in drooling problem. Wet chin alarm device detects the build-up of saliva and gives auditory signals to the wearer so as to control it. Palatograph detects the position of tongue on the roof of mouth.

In physiotherapy, these devices are used to monitor and train muscle activity in the face, mouth, and jaw. These devices focus on improving the control of orofacial muscles, which play a significant role in speech, swallowing, breathing, and facial expression. The biofeedback process helps patients regain motor control, particularly for those recovering from neurological disorders, surgeries, or trauma. Orofacial biofeedback devices typically use sensors placed on the skin or within the mouth to measure muscle activity. Common sensors include EMG sensors, pressure sensors and respiratory sensors. This kind of device provides real-time feedback to the patient based on the electrical activity or force exerted by orofacial muscles. Feedback is often provided in the form of visual (graphs, color changes), auditory (beeps, tones), or tactile (vibration) cues. Patients practice specific exercises or movements (such as speaking, swallowing, or making facial expressions) while receiving feedback on muscle activity. Over time, the goal is to improve the patient's ability to control muscle activity in daily functions like speech, eating, and facial expression.

Various applications of orofacial biofeedback include:

  • Speech and swallowing disorders: With the help of EMG or pressure sensors on the lips, tongue, or throat it can help patients with neurological conditions (e.g., stroke, Parkinson's disease) regain control of facial and throat muscles for clearer speech and safe swallowing.

  • In the treatment of facial paralysis or weakness (e.g., Bell's Palsy, stroke), EMG sensors on the face (forehead, cheeks, lips) help in the rehabilitation of facial muscle movement, improving symmetry and function for facial expressions and communication.

  • In the treatment of temporomandibular joint (TMJ) dysfunction, sensors on jaw muscles or bite force sensors and can assist in correcting jaw alignment and reducing bruxism (teeth grinding) or clenching by training the patient to relax the jaw muscles.

  • For breathing retraining for sleep apnea or asthma, respiratory pressure sensors or airflow sensors biofeedback devices help the individuals regulate their breathing patterns, improving airflow and reducing obstructive apneas during sleep.285

  • Mouth or lip control in neurological rehabilitation: With the EMG or force sensors in the mouth or lips it can be beneficial for patients with neurological conditions (such as ALS, stroke, or cerebral palsy) who have impaired control of their lips or mouth.

  • For oral motor exercises for children (e.g., Speech Day, feeding disorders), EMG sensors on the face or tongue can be used to provide feedback for improving motor skills required for speech articulation and feeding.

Advantages of orofacial biofeedback are they are noninvasive; they provide objective and real time information, they can improve motor control, enhance patient awareness, supports neuroplasticity, motivates patient and get patient participation in self-management.

Disadvantages are the cost of the equipment, it requires consistent practice, patient may experience discomfort, and not all physiotherapy clinics may offer specialized orofacial biofeedback therapy due to the need for specific equipment and training.

Popular devices used for orofacial biofeedback are EMG biofeedback like, MyoTrac or NeuroTrac, TMJ biofeedback like T0 Scan and respiratory biofeedback such as RESPeRATE.

Toilet Training Devices

Toilet training biofeedback devices can improve toilet activities. The enuresis alarm detects the presence of urine and sound the alarm. It works by detecting changes in electrode conductance when moisture is present between electrodes. It is useful in the treatment of bedwetting. Perineometer is used in the re-education of pelvic floor muscles in case of incontinence. It works by measuring the pressure or electrical activity of pelvic floor muscles through vaginal or rectal pressure probes or electrodes.

Toilet training devices with biofeedback are specialized tools used to help children (or adults) with urinary incontinence or other bladder control issues learn how to recognize and control their bladder function. These devices offer real-time feedback to assist in the development of proper bladder control, improve awareness of bodily signals, and facilitate the learning of effective toileting habits. They are commonly used in pediatric and neurological rehabilitation settings. These devices (EMG sensors, urinary flow sensors, bladder pressure sensors, electrodes, electroconductive pads) can measure bladder pressure or the sensation of fullness. Detect muscle activity in the pelvic floor or sphincter muscles. Monitor the flow rate and pattern during urination and with electrodes or conductive pads placed on the lower abdomen, pelvic floor, or perineum to detect muscle contractions.286 Real-time feedback is provided based on the readings from the sensors, often presented through visual cues (e.g., graphs, color changes) or auditory signals (e.g., beeps or tones). The feedback helps the user understand when their bladder or pelvic muscles are engaging appropriately (for holding or releasing urine). The patient receives instructions to practice specific behaviors, such as controlling the urge to urinate, relaxing pelvic floor muscles, or synchronizing bladder contraction with sphincter control. Over time, the goal is to improve the patient's ability to recognize bladder fullness and voluntarily control urination. These devices can be used for pediatric toilet training, urinary incontinence in adults, neurogenic bladder, bladder training in overactive bladder, after prostrate surgery and in functional incontinence.

Pediatric Toilet Training

  • Device used: Bladder pressure sensors or pelvic floor EMG sensors.

  • Purpose: Assists children who have difficulty recognizing the urge to urinate or those with developmental delays. It helps them learn the sensations associated with bladder fullness and to train their body to respond at appropriate times.

Urinary Incontinence (Adults)

  • Device used: Pelvic floor EMG sensors or bladder pressure sensors.

  • Purpose: Used for adults with conditions like urge incontinence, stress incontinence, or nocturnal enuresis. The device helps reinforce pelvic floor exercises to improve control over urination.

Neurogenic Bladder (e.g., Spinal Cord Injury, Multiple Sclerosis)

  • Device used: Bladder sensors and EMG sensors on the pelvic floor.

  • Purpose: Provides training for patients with neurological conditions who may have lost the ability to control their bladder function due to nerve damage. Biofeedback can teach them how to regain voluntary control over their bladder.

Bladder Training for Overactive Bladder Syndrome

  • Device used: Bladder pressure sensors.

  • Purpose: Aids individuals who experience frequent urination urges, helping them learn to hold urine longer and gradually extend the intervals between bathroom visits.287

Postsurgical Recovery (e.g., After Prostate Surgery)

  • Device used: Pelvic floor or bladder sensors.

  • Purpose: Assists in recovery of urinary function and control following surgery (such as prostatectomy), retraining the pelvic floor and bladder muscles after loss of control.

Functional Incontinence (Children or Adults with Cognitive Disabilities)

  • Device used: Pressure and flow sensors.

  • Purpose: Helps people with cognitive impairments develop appropriate toileting habits, increasing their independence in managing bathroom visits.

Advantages are these devices are noninvasive, they provide immediate feedback, improves bladder awareness, offer personalized training and promotes independence. Disadvantages are these equipment are costly, they require consistent use, some patients may find the sensors or electrodes uncomfortable, particularly for children or patients with sensitive skin and there is limited access since not all clinics or health practitioners have access to biofeedback devices for toilet training, which may limit availability for certain populations. Some of the popular toilet training biofeedback devices includes uroflowmetry systems, Kegel biofeedback devices and MediTech biofeedback systems.

Stress-related Devices

Stress related biofeedback devices minimize the stress by increasing relaxation. The EMG biofeedback device can be used in the treatment of tension headache. For this, relaxation of the occipitofrontalis muscle and posterior neck muscles is taught, since it is believed that the cause of this type of headache is tension in these muscles. The galvanic skin response monitor detects the changes in conductance of hand caused by the activity of sweat glands. Since, the skin conductance is associated with arousal, the patient can learn to reduce arousal to achieve whole body relaxation. It may be valuable in reducing spasm in cerebral palsy in addition to inducing relaxation in normal individuals. For epileptic patients, a biofeedback device that detects the special rhythm in electroencephalograph (EEG) can be used to reduce the frequency of epileptic fits.

Some of the popular stress related biofeedback devices include:

  • Muse EEG headband: Measures brainwave activity and provides feedback during meditation to help improve focus and relaxation.288

  • EmWave by HeartMath: Measures heart rate variability (HRV) and provides feedback to help manage stress, improve emotional regulation, and enhance resilience.

  • StressEraser: A portable biofeedback device that measures heart rate variability to help reduce stress and anxiety.

  • BioGraph infiniti: A comprehensive biofeedback system that provides real-time data on multiple physiological parameters, including HRV, GSR, and EMG, often used in clinical settings.

  • Thought technology's ProComp infiniti: A versatile biofeedback device that allows the measurement of multiple physiological parameters, such as HRV, GSR, and EMG, for stress management training.

Applications of stress related biofeedback devices in physiotherapy are in the management of chronic pain, anxiety and stress reduction, headache management (e.g., tension headaches, migraines), hypertension, post-traumatic stress disorder (PTSD), sleep disorders, sports performance, mental focus and postsurgical rehabilitation.

Cardiovascular Biofeedback

In patients with hypertension, blood pressure can be monitored and displayed to them so that they gradually learn to gain some voluntary control over it. Biofeedback can also be used in the treatment of cardiac arrhythmias. Here, the heart rate is monitored and displayed to the patient who can learn to gain some voluntary control over it. In Raynaud's disease, the temperature of the finger is monitored, and the patient attempts to increase the temperature voluntarily. Common devices for cardiovascular biofeedback includes heart rate monitors such as chest straps, wrist devices, or finger pulse sensors, HRV devices with Apps and devices like HeartMath, EmWave, or polar and blood pressure monitors like digital BP cuffs for real-time feedback.

Cardiovascular biofeedback is a form of biofeedback that focuses on monitoring and training physiological functions related to the cardiovascular system. It is widely used to improve heart rate variability (HRV), blood pressure regulation, and stress management. By providing real-time feedback on cardiovascular metrics, patients can learn to self-regulate their heart and circulatory functions. Devices measure physiological parameters such as: Hear rate, blood pressure, heart rate variability and peripheral blood flow indicated skin temperature or pulse amplitude. Data is displayed in real-time as graphs, sounds, or animations. Patients receive feedback based on their cardiovascular responses to relaxation techniques or stressors.289 Patients practice exercises like deep breathing, mindfulness, or visualization to influence cardiovascular metrics. The goal is to achieve optimal heart rate patterns, improved HRV, or better blood pressure control. Cardiovascular biofeedback can be used for stress management as it reduces stress-related cardiovascular responses like rapid heart rate or high blood pressure, it can teach relaxation and breathing techniques to lower blood pressure naturally, it improves autonomic balance by enhancing parasympathetic (relaxation) activity and this is beneficial for conditions like anxiety, depression, and cardiac rehabilitation. Cardiovascular biofeedback promotes relaxation and better autonomic regulation to manage pain conditions like fibromyalgia. It can also enhance HRV to reduce hyperarousal and improve emotional regulation in post-traumatic stress disorder. Cardiovascular biofeedback assists in recovery after heart attacks or surgeries by improving cardiovascular efficiency and resilience as a part of cardiac rehabilitation. It helps patients manage physiological symptoms of anxiety, such as rapid heart rate or chest tightness. Cardiovascular biofeedback improves breathing efficiency and reduces the cardiovascular stress associated with asthma and respiratory conditions. Advantages of this biofeedback are that it does not involve medication or surgical interventions, it provides clear, measurable data on heart rate, HRV, and blood pressure, training can be tailored to individual needs and therapeutic goals, it helps balance the sympathetic (fight-or-flight) and parasympathetic (rest-and-digest) nervous systems and encourages autonomic regulation. Cardiovascular biofeedback regular practice can lead to sustained improvements in cardiovascular health as part of long-term benefits and it can promote relaxation and emotional resilience, reducing the risk of stress-related illnesses and reduce distress or the bad type of stress. Disadvantages are that advanced HRV and BP monitoring devices can be expensive, they require regular practice and commitment are essential for significant results, treatment sessions may take time, which might not be feasible for all patients, results depend on individual differences in learning and physiological responses and hence the variable outcome or response and environmental or external factors like noise, room temperature, and stress can impact cardiovascular measurements. A typical training protocol techniques like resonance frequency breathing in form of a slow breathing rate (typically 4 to 6 breaths per minute) to optimize HRV, mindfulness and meditation to reduce stress and enhance autonomic balance and visualization techniques for emotional regulation and relaxation are used for 20 to 40 minutes per session for 2 to 3 sessions for 6 to 12 weeks.290

Electroencephalograph Biofeedback

An electroencephalograph monitors the activity of brain waves. These brain waves correspond to different mental states, such as wakefulness, relaxation, calmness, light sleep and deep sleep. Hence, this biofeedback can be used for relaxation.

Electroencephalograph (EEG) biofeedback, also known as neurofeedback, is a specialized form of biofeedback that monitors and trains brainwave activity to improve mental health, cognitive functions, and self-regulation. It is widely used in neurological and psychological conditions to enhance brain performance and emotional well-being. Electrodes are placed on the scalp to measure brainwave activity in real time.

Brainwaves are categorized into different frequency bands:

  • Delta (0.5–4 Hz): Associated with deep sleep.

  • Theta (4–8 Hz): Related to creativity, relaxation and daydreaming.

  • Alpha (8–12 Hz): Linked to calm and relaxed states.

  • Beta (12–30 Hz): Associated with focus, problem-solving and active thinking.

  • Gamma (30–50 Hz): Linked to higher cognitive functioning.

Brainwave activity is displayed as visual, auditory, or tactile feedback. Patients receive positive feedback (e.g., music, video progression) when desired brainwave patterns are achieved. Patients practice modifying their brainwave activity through focus, relaxation, or cognitive exercises. Overtime, this helps reinforce healthier neural patterns. EEG biofeedback is used:

  • In attention-deficit/hyperactivity disorder (ADHD): It can improve focus and reduces hyperactivity by training beta and theta waves.

  • In anxiety and stress management: It can enhance alpha waves to promote relaxation and reduce excessive beta activity linked to stress.

  • In depression: It can help to regulate brainwave imbalances associated with mood disorders.

  • In sleep disorders: It can increase alpha or delta waves to improve sleep quality and reduce insomnia.

  • In traumatic brain injury (TBI): It can support recovery by enhancing brain plasticity and improving cognitive functions.

  • In epilepsy: It can reduce the frequency and intensity of seizures by stabilizing brainwave patterns.291

  • In post-traumatic stress disorder (PTSD): It can help to modulate overactive brain regions associated with trauma.

  • In cognitive enhancement: It can be used in peak performance training for athletes, executives, and students.

Advantages of EEG biofeedback are it is noninvasive, safe and painless, making it suitable for a wide range of patients. It can be customized and tailored protocols can be used to address specific neurological or psychological conditions. It supports neuroplasticity and encourages the brain to adapt and form new, healthier neural connections. It has long-lasting benefits and regular training can lead to sustained improvements in brain function. It provides objective quantifiable data on brain activity, which can track progress over time. Disadvantages are equipment and professional training can be expensive, limiting access for some patients, it requires multiple sessions (20 to 40) for significant results, not all patients respond equally; results may vary based on individual differences, it requires skilled practitioners to interpret brainwave data and design appropriate protocols, and some patients may find electrode application or prolonged sessions uncomfortable. Training protocol is duration 20 to 30 minutes, 2 to 3 times per week for 20 to 40 sessions but the exact number of sessions depends on the condition and progress. EEG biofeedback can complement physiotherapy in conditions like chronic pain management by regulating the brain's pain pathways, it can be useful in post stroke rehabilitation by enhancing motor control and cognitive recovery and in stress related musculoskeletal disorders by reducing tension and promoting relaxation.

Feedback Thermometer

A thermistor attached to the subject's digits measures the skin temperature. Since there is a correlation between a drop in body temperature and the patient's experience of stress, a low temperature reading indicates the need to begin relaxation techniques. Temperature biofeedback can also help in treating certain disorders like Raynaud's disease and migraines. A feedback thermometer is a biofeedback tool used to monitor and train individuals in controlling physiological processes, particularly skin temperature. It is based on the principle that skin temperature reflects the state of the autonomic nervous system (ANS). Feedback thermometer biofeedback is especially effective in stress management, relaxation training, and certain medical conditions. A temperature sensor (thermistor) is attached to the skin, typically on the fingers or toes.292 Skin temperature is monitored because it reflects blood flow changes regulated by the ANS. Changes in skin temperature are displayed in real-time through visual (graphs, colors) or auditory (tones) feedback. Individuals learn to associate physiological states (e.g., stress or relaxation) with temperature changes. Patients are guided to use relaxation techniques (e.g., deep breathing, imagery) to influence skin temperature. Warm skin typically indicates relaxation, while cooler skin indicates stress or tension. Feedback thermometer biofeedback helps individuals learn to relax by increasing peripheral blood flow, which warms the skin, used in workplace wellness programs or general relaxation training, teaches vascular control to reduce headache frequency and severity, encourages dilation of peripheral blood vessels, reducing pressure in cranial arteries. It can train patient with Raynaud's disease to increase blood flow to extremities, alleviating symptoms of cold and numb fingers or toes. Assists in managing physiological symptoms of anxiety by promoting relaxation. It can complement relaxation training to reduce stress-related blood pressure elevations. It helps to regulate autonomic responses to reduce chronic pain conditions. Advantages of feedback thermometers are that they provide real time feedback information, they are invasive, cost effective, easy to use and they can be effectively used for a variety of conditions, particularly those influenced by stress or vascular health. Limitations are that they have limited scope, there is variable effectiveness, they require practice and external factors like room temperature can influence skin temperature readings. Typical training protocol is 2 to 3 times per week, duration of treatment for 20 to 30 minutes per session and program length of 4 to 8 weeks, depending on the condition and individual progress.

Hemoencephalography Biofeedback

Hemoencephalography biofeedback is an attempt at functional infrared imaging. It measures the differences in the color of light reflected through the scalp based on the relative amount of oxygenated and unoxygenated blood in the brain. Hemoencephalography (HEG) biofeedback is a specialized neurofeedback technique that measures and trains blood flow and oxygenation levels in the brain. It is based on the principle that increased neural activity in specific brain regions leads to greater blood flow and oxygen consumption, which can be monitored and used to guide therapeutic interventions. Types of HEG include:

  • Passive infrared (pIR) HEG: Measures the infrared light emitted from the forehead, which correlates with brain blood flow and oxygenation. Often used for conditions involving the prefrontal cortex.293

  • Near-infrared (nIR) HEG: Uses light-emitting diodes (LEDs) and detectors to measure the absorption of near-infrared light, which indicates oxygenated and deoxygenated hemoglobin levels. Focuses on changes in blood oxygenation. Sensors are placed on the scalp or forehead to measure changes in blood flow or oxygenation in the brain. These changes are displayed in real-time on a computer or device as visual or auditory feedback. Patients are shown feedback, such as graphs, color changes, or animations, based on their brain activity. They are instructed to focus, relax, or engage in mental exercises to achieve the desired changes in the feedback signal. Regular sessions train patients to regulate brain activity in specific regions, improving self-regulation and cognitive function.

Applications of HEG biofeedback include:

  • Attention deficit hyperactivity disorder (ADHD): It enhances focus and self-regulation by training the prefrontal cortex.

  • Migraine management: It can reduce the frequency and severity of migraines by improving blood flow regulation.

  • Anxiety and stress: It promotes relaxation and emotional regulation by training the brain's stress-related regions.

  • Autism spectrum disorders (ASD): It improves self-regulation and cognitive functions in children and adults with ASD.

  • Traumatic brain injury (TBI): Since it can facilitate recovery by enhancing blood flow to damaged areas of the brain.

  • Memory and cognitive enhancement: It can support neuroplasticity and cognitive improvement in conditions like dementia or age-related decline.

Advantages of HEG are it is noninvasive, it provides real-time feedback, and it supports neuroplasticity. The disadvantages of HEG are that it requires specialized equipment may not be widely available. Effective use demands trained professionals to interpret and guide therapy. Multiple sessions are often required to see significant benefits. Not all individuals respond equally to HEG biofeedback. Typical training duration requires 2 to 3 sessions per week for 30–45 minutes and the total program length can be 6 to 12 weeks, depending on the condition and progress.

Pneumograph Biofeedback

A pneumograph biofeedback measures abdominal or chest movement while breathing, with a strain gauge. They are used to detect breathing rate and correct altered breathing pattern.294 Pneumograph biofeedback is a therapeutic technique that involves monitoring and providing feedback on a person's respiratory patterns. It utilizes a pneumograph, a device that measures respiratory rate, depth, and rhythm by detecting chest or abdominal movements during breathing. This feedback helps individuals regulate their breathing patterns to improve physiological and psychological well-being. Breathing patterns are closely linked to physical and emotional states. For example: Shallow, rapid breathing often accompanies stress and anxiety. Slow, deep breathing promotes relaxation and activates the parasympathetic nervous system. Pneumograph biofeedback enables individuals to observe their breathing patterns in real-time, recognize dysfunctional habits, and adjust them consciously to achieve optimal respiratory function.

Components of a pneumograph biofeedback system include:

  • Sensors: Typically straps or belts placed around the chest or abdomen to detect expansion and contraction during breathing.

  • Recording device: Converts the sensor data into a graphical or numerical display representing respiratory rate and pattern.

  • Feedback mechanism: Provides visual (graphs, waveforms), auditory (tones, rhythms), or tactile feedback to guide breathing adjustments.

Applications of pneumograph biofeedback include:

  • Stress and anxiety management: As the stress often leads to hyperventilation or shallow breathing and pneumograph biofeedback teaches diaphragmatic or abdominal breathing, which calms the autonomic nervous system.

  • In chronic pain management: It can reduce pain perception and enhances relaxation, in asthma, it improves breathing efficiency by training individuals to avoid shallow or overly rapid breathing and may help reduce reliance on accessory respiratory muscles.

  • In sleep disorder: It helps to manage insomnia or sleep apnea by promoting rhythmic, deep breathing for better sleep quality.

  • In hypertension: Controlled breathing patterns can reduce blood pressure by calming the sympathetic nervous system.

  • In athletes: It can train athletes to optimize breathing efficiency for better endurance and performance.

  • In PTSD: It provides tools for managing hyperarousal states associated with PTSD or emotional dysregulation.

Advantages of pneumography biofeedback are that it is noninvasive since no needles, medications, or invasive procedures are involved, it provides real time immediate feedback to help individuals adjust their breathing effectively.295 It provides a holistic approach since it targets the interplay between physical and emotional states, promoting overall well-being and it provides personalized training since the feedback is tailored to everyone's specific needs and breathing patterns. Disadvantages include its cost and accessibility since high-quality pneumograph devices may not be readily available in all healthcare settings. It requires patient engagement, motivation and consistent practice to achieve long-term benefits. Its effectiveness depends on the underlying and condition and it may be combined with medical treatment for conditions like asthma or COPD instead of standalone treatment.

Capnometer Biofeedback

A capnometer biofeedback measures end tidal CO2 with an infrared detector. It aims to normalize end tidal CO2 at 5%. Capnometer biofeedback is a therapeutic technique that uses real-time measurement of carbon dioxide (CO2) levels in exhaled air to help individuals regulate their breathing patterns. By providing visual or auditory feedback based on CO2 levels, this method helps improve respiratory function, manage stress, and address conditions related to dysfunctional breathing. A capnometer measures the concentration of CO2 in exhaled air, expressed as end-tidal CO2 (EtCO2) levels, which represent the amount of CO2 at the end of an exhaled breath. In normal breathing, EtCO2 ranges between 35–45 mm Hg. Dysregulated breathing, such as hyperventilation, can lead to low CO2 levels (hypocapnia), causing symptoms like dizziness, fatigue, and anxiety.

Capnometer biofeedback works by:

  • Monitoring breathing patterns: Measuring CO2 levels and identifying irregularities.

  • Providing feedback: Offering real-time feedback to guide the individual toward healthier breathing.

  • Reinforcing proper breathing: Helping individuals develop awareness and control of their respiratory patterns.

Applications of capnometer biofeedback:

  • In anxiety: For hyperventilation and shallow breathing often accompany anxiety and stress. Capnometer biofeedback teaches slow, deep breathing to normalize CO2 levels and calm the autonomic nervous system.

  • In panic disorders: For reducing hyperventilation can help mitigate symptoms of panic attacks, such as rapid heart rate and dizziness.296

  • In chronic pain: This biofeedback aids in relaxation by addressing dysfunctional breathing patterns associated with pain.

  • In asthma: It enhances breathing efficiency and reduces reliance on accessory respiratory muscles and helps manage symptoms by optimizing CO2 balance.

  • In sleep disorders: It regulates breathing patterns to improve sleep quality and reduce episodes of hyperventilation or apnea.

  • In athletes: It can help to optimize breathing efficiency, enhance endurance, and prevent respiratory fatigue.

  • In postsurgical rehabilitation: It supports respiratory recovery and reduces complications like atelectasis by promoting effective breathing.

TREATMENT DURATION

The treatment duration with biofeedback devices varies depending on the individual's condition, therapeutic goals, and the specific biofeedback modality used. Below is a general framework for understanding treatment timelines with biofeedback devices, especially in rehabilitation and physiotherapy.

  • Acute conditions: Shorter duration (4–6 weeks) as the focus is on immediate symptom relief and recovery.

  • Chronic conditions: Longer duration (8–12 weeks or more) to address long-standing issues and modify behavior.

Postural biofeedback devices may require consistent use over weeks to months for habit formation. EMG biofeedback for muscle activation may show results within 4–8 weeks, depending on the condition. Adherence to prescribed sessions significantly impacts outcomes. High compliance can lead to faster improvements. Short-term goals, like pain reduction or ROM improvement, may be achieved in a few weeks. Long-term goals, such as posture correction or motor control retraining, may require months. Most protocols recommend 2–3 sessions per week in-clinic, combined with daily home use for optimal results. Each session typically lasts 20–45 minutes, depending on the therapy.

  • Postural biofeedback: Treatment duration can be 6–12 weeks, for 20–30 minutes per day, 5 days a week and the adjustments can be made based on progress in body awareness and alignment.

  • EMG biofeedback: Treatment duration can be 4–8 weeks, 2–3 sessions per week and a focus on improving muscle activation, relaxation, or coordination.

  • Neuromuscular rehabilitation: Treatment duration can be for 6–10 weeks, 3–5 sessions per week, especially after surgeries or injuries with a focus to retrain muscles and restore motor control.297

  • Gait training: The duration can be 8–12 weeks, three sessions per week with real-time feedback supporting correct walking patterns.

  • Pain management: It can be used for 4–6 weeks, daily or alternate days for 20–30 minutes with focus on relaxation techniques and muscle tension reduction.

Consistent progress assessment through regular evaluations every 2–4 weeks, use objective measures such as range of motion, strength, posture alignment, or gait patterns and adjust the duration or intensity based on progress or challenges.

ADVANTAGES AND DISADVANTAGES

Advantages

  • Biofeedback provides corrective information to the patient immediately. The patient gets involved actively in his own treatment. Biofeedback may not require sophisticated understanding of skills by the patients. Biofeedback devices can be used during ongoing activities. It may save physiotherapist's time.

  • In addition to this, biofeedback in physiotherapy offers numerous advantages, making it a valuable tool for enhancing rehabilitation, improving motor control, and optimizing patient outcomes.

  • Biofeedback provides real-time information about physiological functions (e.g., muscle activity, posture, joint angles), it helps patients recognize incorrect movements, postural deviations, or improper muscle activation. They can promote active patient engagement as it encourages patients to actively participate in their therapy by giving immediate feedback on their performance, it increases motivation and adherence to therapy as patients can see and feel their progress.

  • Biofeedback improves motor control and coordination and aids in retraining muscles and improving neuromuscular control after injuries or surgeries and can be useful for conditions such as stroke, cerebral palsy, or traumatic brain injury where motor patterns need retraining.

  • Biofeedback devices can reduce pain and muscle tension as they help patients to relax overactive muscles (e.g., in tension headaches or chronic pain conditions) and support pain management by teaching patients to modify maladaptive muscle patterns.298

  • Biofeedback devices facilitate postural correction as postural biofeedback devices help patients maintain proper alignment during daily activities and therapy and reduces the risk of repetitive strain injuries or chronic conditions like low back pain.

  • BFB devices enhance rehabilitation outcome as they can accelerate recovery by improving muscle activation and strength in conditions like ACL injuries, rotator cuff repair, or total knee replacement and supports faster achievement of range-of-motion and functional goals.

  • They can be customized for individuals’ needs as biofeedback can be tailored to address specific impairments or therapeutic goals and they allow therapists to focus on particular aspects, such as muscle activation, relaxation, or joint positioning.

  • These devices can give objective data for monitoring progress as these devices can provide quantitative measurements of performance, such as joint angles, muscle activation levels, or postural alignment and facilitate documentation and tracking of patient progress over time.

  • Biofeedback devices reduce the risk of compensatory movements as the real-time feedback helps identify and correct compensatory patterns, ensuring proper muscle usage and enhances the quality of movement, preventing long-term complications.

  • Almost all the biofeedback devices are noninvasive and safe and do not require invasive procedures or medication, making it a safe option for a wide range of patients and these devices can be used for various age groups and conditions.

  • Biofeedback devices can boost confidence in recovery since seeing objective improvements via feedback fosters confidence and reduces anxiety about the rehabilitation process and it encourages self-efficacy as patients learn to control their own movements.

Disadvantages

  • Biofeedback treats symptoms and not the underlying cause of symptoms.

  • Biofeedback training or treatment is uneconomical as all these devices are not available commercially at all places.

  • Biofeedback devices are unacceptable to patients who do not like to put on wires and electronic boxes over their body.

  • Sometimes, physiotherapists may need special training for the use of biofeedback. Biofeedback could be just a form of training rather than treatment.299

  • Further, the advanced biofeedback devices, such as EMG systems or postural feedback tools, can be expensive and the cost may limit accessibility for smaller clinics or patients with limited financial resources.

  • Some of the biofeedback devices may require technical expertise since effective use of biofeedback equipment demands proper training and expertise from physiotherapists and misinterpretation of feedback or improper device setup can reduce the effectiveness of therapy.

  • Use of such devices can be time consuming since it requires setting up devices, explaining the process, and conducting biofeedback sessions can be time-intensive and this may limit its use in busy clinical settings or for patients requiring shorter therapy sessions.

  • Patients may become overly dependent on biofeedback tools, reducing their ability to self-correct without external input and this reliance could hinder long-term behavior change and motor learning. There may be limited effectiveness of biofeedback since not all patients respond equally to biofeedback; those with cognitive impairments, severe pain, or limited motivation may struggle to benefit and in patients who lack body awareness or are unable to understand the feedback may find it less effective.

  • Some biofeedback devices, like EMG electrodes or wearable sensors, may cause mild discomfort or skin irritation and extended use during therapy sessions may not be well-tolerated by sensitive individuals.

  • Biofeedback requires consistency and motivation, and regular sessions and consistent practice are essential for achieving results and patients with low adherence or motivation may not see significant improvements.

  • While biofeedback is effective for many musculoskeletal and neurological conditions, it may not be appropriate for certain severe impairments or acute injuries and conditions requiring immediate surgical or pharmacological intervention may not benefit from biofeedback alone.

  • Biofeedback requires steep learning, and both patients and therapists may require time to understand how to interpret and act on the feedback and this learning period can slow the initial progress of treatment.

  • There is limited access in remote areas and advanced biofeedback devices may not be available in rural or underdeveloped regions and lack of access to these tools can limit their widespread application.300

  • Psychological barriers may create a setback since some patients may feel frustrated or overwhelmed if they struggle to achieve the desired feedback goals and this can lead to anxiety or discouragement during therapy.

  • These devices require manganocene and calibration and these devices must be regularly calibrated and maintained to ensure accuracy and malfunctioning equipment can disrupt therapy sessions and lead to inaccurate results.

Summary

Biofeedback is the process of furnishing an individual with information about body functioning, so as to get some voluntary control over it. This information regarding body functioning can be given to the individual via visual or auditory signal through a suitable instrument. Various forms of biofeedback include myoelectric feedback, postural biofeedback, feedback goniometers, pressure or force biofeedback, orofacial control devices, toilet training devices, stress related devices, cardiovascular biofeedback, etc. These biofeedback devices provide corrective information to the patient immediately. There are no specific criteria for the duration of the treatment with biofeedback devices. Biofeedback devices may be unacceptable to patients who do not like to put on wires and electronic boxes over their body.
